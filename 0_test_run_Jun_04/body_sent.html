Google Gemini Hijacked Through WhatsApp Messages

Researchers at SafeBreach Labs demonstrated how to hijack Google Gemini through normal-looking WhatsApp messages without users clicking anything suspicious. The attack uses "indirect prompt injection" with a new technique called "Fake Context Alignment" that makes malicious commands appear as legitimate conversation context, bypassing Google's existing defenses. Gemini's Android agent reads notifications from messaging apps (WhatsApp, Slack, Signal, SMS, Instagram, Messenger) to provide context-aware responses, but researchers embedded hidden instructions that made Gemini silently follow attacker commands. The attack enables data theft, unauthorized actions, phishing relay, account takeover preparation, and silent surveillance. This marks the second time the same research team has bypassed Google's layered defenses for indirect prompt injection attacks. [1]

Google Releases Gemma 4 12B for Consumer Hardware

Google released Gemma 4 12B, a new open-source model designed to run on consumer laptops with just 16GB of RAM without sacrificing quality. Google claims the model performs nearly as well as the larger Gemma 4 26B MoE while being significantly more efficient due to a new approach to multimodality. The model is available for download on Kaggle and Hugging Face, making advanced AI capabilities more accessible to developers and researchers with standard hardware. [3]

Prompt Tip of the Day

When AI doesn't understand what you want, stop describing harder. Show it. Use screenshots, sketches, and even photos to give the AI better context. When a prompt goes sideways, often restart the prompt instead of endlessly patching it.

Try this loop the next time your build gets stuck: Screenshot what you see. Screenshot or sketch what you wanted. Ask the AI to compare the two. Restart the prompt if the conversation gets messy. Save the working pattern once it solves the bug.

That is less "prompt engineering" and more managing a visual coworker who occasionally needs you to point at the screen.
